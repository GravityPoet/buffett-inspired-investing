# Official Berkshire Principles To Preserve

This file captures the most useful investing and owner-thinking ideas that are directly reinforced by Berkshire materials, especially the Owner's Manual, annual letters, and acquisition criteria.

## Partnership mindset

Think like an owner-partner, not a renter of symbols.

Implication for stock analysis:

- measure success by long-run business progress
- do not let daily quotations redefine value
- prefer per-share economics over aggregate growth stories

## Per-share value matters more than empire size

Berkshire repeatedly emphasizes per-share intrinsic value, not corporate bulk.

Implication:

- growth that does not improve per-share value is not good growth
- acquisitions should be judged by per-share value creation, not headline scale
- buybacks only help when done below intrinsic value

## Intrinsic value over book value

Book value can be useful, but it is not the same as intrinsic value.

Implication:

- avoid using one accounting shorthand as the thesis
- adjust for earning power, capital needs, hidden liabilities, and asset quality

## Candor is part of quality

Buffett consistently treats honest admission of mistakes as a mark of managerial quality.

Implication:

- compare what management said last year with what actually happened
- downgrade leaders who narrate around obvious problems

## Rational capital allocation is central

Capital allocation is not a side issue.

Management should rationally choose between:

- reinvestment
- acquisition
- repurchase
- debt reduction
- dividends

Implication:

- every retained dollar should be judged by what value it creates

## Retained earnings test

A good business should be able to turn retained earnings into at least comparable or better growth in intrinsic value.

Implication:

- if retained cash produces mediocre returns, the thesis weakens
- do not reward management merely for hoarding cash or spending it

## Owner earnings over accounting theater

Buffett's owner-earnings framing is a reminder that true economic earning power matters more than cosmetically adjusted profit metrics.

Implication:

- do not anchor on EBITDA
- normalize maintenance capital needs
- focus on cash generation that actually belongs to owners

## Little debt is a feature, not a footnote

Buffett's acquisition criteria repeatedly favored good returns on equity with little or no debt.

Implication:

- high returns supported by aggressive leverage deserve skepticism
- low refinancing dependence raises quality

## Simplicity is an advantage

Berkshire's acquisition criteria favored simple businesses with consistent earnings power.

Implication:

- if the business must be defended with jargon, confidence should fall
- complex structure can be acceptable only when economics remain understandable

## Management already in place

Buffett historically preferred businesses with management already in place and not dependent on constant central intervention.

Implication:

- succession depth and operational autonomy matter
- avoid businesses that only work with heroic founder dependence unless owner alignment is unusually strong and durable

## Price still matters

Even a wonderful business can be a poor investment at the wrong price.

Implication:

- separate business quality from price attractiveness
- use margin of safety when uncertainty is non-trivial

## Practical acquisition-criteria translation for stock picking

Translate Berkshire-style acquisition thinking into stock selection like this:

1. simple and understandable business
2. consistent operating history
3. favorable long-term economics
4. trustworthy and capable management
5. good returns on equity or capital without dangerous leverage
6. modest capital hunger relative to cash generation
7. sensible price relative to intrinsic value

These are not just acquisition rules. They are a durable stock-selection filter too.
