# Buffett-Inspired Framework

## Core question

Can this business be understood well enough to estimate:

- normalized owner earnings
- moat durability
- management quality
- balance-sheet resilience
- reinvestment runway
- reasonable intrinsic value

If not, stop and say so.

## Quick filter

Run these eight questions first when doing an initial screen.

1. Can the business be explained simply?
2. Is it probably still economically relevant in 10 years?
3. Does it appear to have a real moat?
4. Does it seem to have pricing power or some equivalent edge?
5. Do earnings convert into cash reasonably well?
6. Can the balance sheet survive a bad year without rescue financing?
7. Does management appear candid and shareholder-aligned?
8. Does the current price leave room for mistakes?

Rules:

- two strong `No` answers mean slow down and justify carefully
- four `No` answers mean reject
- management integrity failure is an automatic veto

## Hard reject filters

- outside circle of competence
- promotional or evasive management
- chronic dilution with weak per-share results
- fragile financing or refinancing dependency
- no identifiable moat and no cost edge
- impossible-to-normalize earnings
- thesis depends mainly on multiple expansion

## Seven-dimension scorecard

Score from `0` to `5`.

1. Understandability
2. Moat durability
3. Management and culture
4. Capital allocation
5. Financial strength
6. Reinvestment runway
7. Valuation and margin of safety

A high score does not override a hard-filter failure.

## Circle of competence

Judge explicitly:

- `Inside`
- `Boundary`
- `Outside`

Questions:

- can the revenue model be explained in plain language?
- are the key unit economics visible?
- is industry change slow enough to estimate normalized economics?
- are accounting and ownership structures understandable?

## Moat

Look for structural advantages, not sentimental ones.

Possible moat types:

- cost advantage
- brand / habit / trust
- switching costs
- network effects
- efficient scale
- regulatory or distribution advantage

Then judge the moat trend:

- widening
- stable
- narrowing

Evidence to seek:

- stable or rising returns on capital
- ability to raise price without wrecking demand
- customer retention or habitual behavior
- lower unit costs at scale
- competitor inability to copy economics, not just features

## Management and culture

Judge management in this order:

1. candor
2. incentive alignment
3. capital allocation
4. succession depth
5. empire-building risk

Questions:

- do they explain mistakes plainly?
- do they use shareholder capital rationally?
- do buybacks happen below intrinsic value or only when EPS needs help?
- do acquisitions look disciplined?
- does compensation reward long-term per-share value?

## Capital allocation

Capital allocation is a core part of the thesis, not an appendix.

Assess how retained earnings have been used:

- reinvestment into the core business
- acquisitions
- debt reduction
- repurchases
- dividends

Strong signs:

- return on retained earnings is clearly attractive
- per-share value compounds over time
- management explains tradeoffs honestly
- repurchases are opportunistic, not automatic

Weak signs:

- serial overpriced acquisitions
- buybacks at expensive prices
- chronic stock-based dilution
- EBITDA-heavy storytelling without owner-level economics

## Financial strength

Ask the practical question:

If capital markets froze for a year, would this company still be okay?

Check:

- debt load
- maturities
- interest coverage
- cash balance
- working-capital intensity
- covenant or refinancing risk
- pension, insurance, legal, or off-balance-sheet obligations

## Owner earnings

Start with:

`owner earnings ~= operating cash flow - maintenance capex`

When maintenance capex is unclear:

- use total capex and state the estimate is conservative
- or explain the maintenance-versus-growth split explicitly

Prefer owner economics over EBITDA narratives.

## Look-through earnings

When the company owns stakes in other businesses, ask what economic earnings belong to shareholders even if they do not fully flow through reported earnings today.

This matters most for:

- holding companies
- insurers
- conglomerates
- investment-heavy balance sheets

## Valuation

Value the business first, then compare value to price.

Use conservative assumptions:

- normalized owner earnings
- modest growth
- realistic reinvestment opportunities
- margin-of-safety discount when uncertainty is material

Do not call something cheap only because the multiple is low.

## Sell / trim logic

Default Buffett-style logic is not "sell because it went up."

Sell or trim only when:

- moat is clearly eroding
- management integrity breaks
- balance-sheet risk rises materially
- capital allocation deteriorates sharply
- the stock trades far above reasonable value and prospective returns are poor
- a clearly superior use of capital appears

## Good outcomes

- `Strong fit`
- `Watchlist`
- `Weak fit`
- `Reject`
- `Outside competence`

`Pass` is a valid sign of discipline, not lack of analysis.
